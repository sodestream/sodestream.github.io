Workshop proposal for EACL 2023: NLP4OrgCom - NLP for Organisational Communication

Brief description

This workshop will discuss NLP for organisational communication – dataset creation, analysis methods, interaction models, tool development and evaluation etc. – across the range of scales (small group meetings to large email lists), media (face-to-face, remote, online), timescales (brief conversations to months-long threads) and modalities (textual, visual, audio, and gesture). It will include both a traditional workshop format and an online hackathon before the event. The Sodestream project will provide resources, funding and the core organisation team.

Motivation and relevance

Interaction and communication are at the heart of every organisation, from small to large, and take many forms: email, group messaging applications, face-to-face and online meetings of various sizes and others. Insights into this process could help make it more efficient and lead to a range of assistive tools. Moreover, the Covid-19 pandemic has caused many previously in-person interactions to move online, leading to changes and an increasing need to understand and improve interaction quality. However, the group interaction involved is often at a scale between the small scales usually studied in NLP for dialogue (both one-to-one and multi-party) and the very large scales usually studied in NLP for social networks. The organisational nature also brings important factors that affect language and communication – explicit or implicit hierarchy, shared or disputed goals, social groupings with competitive or collaborative agendas – well known in other disciplines but not often taken into account in NLP.

The NLP community has looked at various relevant phenomena and tasks (e.g. meeting summarization, argument mining, agreement/disagreement detection, persuasiveness detection), and some relevant datasets have been produced (e.g. the Enron email dataset). Interest in these has increased recently via a number of large projects, such as Opening Up Minds (Farag et al., 2022; Karadzhov et al., 2022), Elitr (Ghosal et al., 2021; Polák et al., 2022), Sodestream (Khare et al., 2022) but there are still relatively few attempts to bring them together, and few resources or approaches to organisational communication in general. This workshop aims to fill this gap to model, analyse and understand overall organisational communication, and encourage collaboration between researchers from diverse backgrounds including computational linguistics, organisational psychology, and computational social science.

Intended audience and diversity

The workshop aims to bring together researchers from NLP, organisational psychology and social science. Invited speakers will come from across these areas; currently confirmed are:

  • Prof. Magda Osman (University of Cambridge): the psychology of decision-making;
  • Dr. Colin Perkins (University of Glasgow): experiences chairing the Internet Research Task Force.

Both workshop tracks are open to anyone interested. We will use our networks to distribute the call and attract diverse participants. We estimate 30-50 participants (partly from ongoing relevant projects). We aim for 20-25 paper submissions. To encourage a diverse set of contributors and participation, we aim to fund some bursaries for registration and travel for attendees who would otherwise be unable to participate. To the hackathon participants, we will provide mentoring and have two mentoring sessions before the workshop.

Format

The workshop will have a dual format: (1) a traditional track with paper submissions, peer reviews and paper presentations, and (2) an active, experimentation-based track where hackathon-type online activities precede the workshop, and teams/individuals present their work in the workshop. Participants can choose to participate in only one or in both roles. The hackathon organisers will provide some data and task suggestions, but participants are free to work on any relevant task or dataset during their hackathon project. Hackathon activities are by design online, while the rest of the workshop will be in person. However, if the COVID situation requires, everything can be held online in coordination with the conference organisers.

After the workshop, we plan to invite selected authors to submit a paper to a special issue of the Dialogue & Discourse journal. The journal submissions would undergo further review, and the paper should be substantially different from the original work.

Timeline: For the traditional workshop track we will follow the timeline proposed by the conference organisers. The call for Hackathon participation will be in December, followed by some time to prepare the tools and data. Hackathon teams will work on their project and report from Mar, 1 to Apr 28. During this period we will hold two hackathon meetings, on Mar 21 and Apr 15, where we will assess the progress of teams and provide feedback if necessary.

Resources: During the workshop, there are no special technical requirements. The organisers will provide data (emails of a large organisation with dialogue structure annotation), tools and support for the hackathon participants. Appropriate public datasets may also be utilised. The data and tools compiled for the hackathon will be made publicly available, to the extent possible.

Financial support: the workshop will be supported by the EPSRC Sodestream project to fund travel and expenses for invited speakers and provide bursaries (see above).
